Black Flame Techniques — Achieving the Perfect Gothic Candle

Fellow artisans of the flame,

I've been experimenting extensively with what I call "Black Flame" candles — candles that burn with an almost otherworldly dark glow instead of the usual bright yellow. After months of trial, I've finally perfected my technique and wanted to share it with the guild.

Materials Needed:

  • Soy wax base (464 or similar low-melt)
  • Activated charcoal powder — food grade
  • Black iron oxide pigment
  • Cotton core wicks (CD series recommended)
  • Dark patchouli + cedarwood fragrance oil blend

The key insight is in the layering technique. Rather than mixing charcoal into the entire candle (which clogs the wick), you create alternating layers of pure black wax and charcoal-infused wax. This creates a mesmerizing visual gradient as the candle burns down.

The iron oxide pigment is what gives the wax that truly deep, light-absorbing black color without interfering with the burn. I use approximately 1 teaspoon per pound of wax.

I'll post detailed step-by-step photos in my next reply. In the meantime — has anyone else experimented with charcoal in their candles? I'd love to compare notes.

— NightWeaver 🦇

Great questions, everyone! Let me answer:

How thick do you pour each layer?

@VelvetStitch — About 1/2 inch per layer works best. I let each layer cool until it's firm but still slightly warm to the touch (around 90°F surface temp), then pour the next. This gives perfect adhesion without mixing.

@IronHide — A candelabra + candle collab would be amazing! Let's chat in DMs about the challenge piece. The copper oxide idea is fascinating — I'll order some this week.

@ShadowCraft — Great question on safety. The amount of charcoal in the layered design is minimal, so the smoke output is negligible compared to a standard candle. That said, always burn in ventilated spaces and never leave unattended. I'd avoid it in very small, sealed rooms.
